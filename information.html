People form their understanding of the world based on the information they consume. The internet enables rapid information sharing, but also amplifies low-quality, misleading, or incomplete signals. Social media platforms prioritize engagement, not accuracy, which increases the visibility of misinformation. Information consumed from these platforms is often internalized and later repeated without clear sourcing.

While not equivalent to absolute truth, rigorous research is the most reliable source of information currently available. Engaging directly with research requires a level of research literacy that many people lack.

Research literacy includes:

  • Locating: Knowing where and how to find relevant research
  • Understanding: Comprehending terminology, methods, and study design
  • Interpreting: Correctly reasoning about results and their implications
  • Evaluating: Assessing methodological quality, bias, and limitations
Low research literacy discourages direct engagement with primary sources. As a result, people rely on simplified, secondary, or algorithmically amplified interpretations. These intermediaries often omit nuance, uncertainty, and context.

When inaccurate or incomplete information is widely adopted, collective models of the world become distorted. Distorted models lead to poor individual decisions and large-scale social consequences, including polarization and conflict. Reducing misinformation requires lowering the barrier between reliable research and everyday understanding.
