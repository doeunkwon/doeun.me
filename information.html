Doeun Kwon Information Privacy

The information we consume plays a significant role in shaping our model of the world. The internet has made it easier to share information, but it also amplifies low-quality signals. Social media platforms prioritize engagement over accuracy, which can increase the visibility of misinformation. As a result, people often internalize and repeat information without verifying its sources. This can lead to a distorted understanding of the world. In contrast, rigorous research provides a reliable source of information, but it requires a certain level of research literacy to engage with. Research literacy involves four key skills:

  1. Locating: Knowing where and how to find relevant research.
  2. Understanding: Comprehending terminology, methods, and study design.
  3. Interpreting: Correctly reasoning about results and their implications.
  4. Evaluating: Assessing quality, bias, and limitations.

However, many people (including me) lack these skills, which can discourage them from engaging directly with primary sources. Instead, they rely on secondary, algorithmically amplified, interpretations, which often omit nuance and context. The consequences of inaccurate information can be severe, leading to poor individual decisions and large-scale social consequences, such as polarization. To mitigate this, it's important to lower the barrier between reliable research and everyday understanding, making it easier for people to access and engage with accurate information.
