Bitcoin

Money, Governance, & Power

We live in an era of digitization. This transition gives investors greater control over their digital assets since these specific type of assets have the unique property of being programmable data types. Of all asset classes, the one currently experiencing the greatest paradigm shift is fiat currency. The ideal electronic cash is superior to fiat because it's a data type that is faster, cheaper, and easier to transfer, while being more durable, trackable, and theft-resistant.

In a way, the US government is lagging because it works under the archaic fiat system. Yet, the government is powerful because it controls the supply of the US dollar, which further controls many aspects of society. Money is the single most influential layer of the societal stack and the government uses money as a tool to manipulate the status of the economy. Now imagine the consequences of the US government establishing and regulating its own digital currency - that is, a superior, programmable, USD. This is all to say, the control of a widely adopted digital currency gives access to an inconceivable amount of data, privacy, and power.

Decentralized Electronic Cash

Bitcoin presents itself as a purely peer-to-peer electronic cash system that is governed by its users rather than a central authority. This decentralization of money addresses a fundamental problem that is unique to digital cash known as the double-spending problem. Since digital cash is just electronic data, it's at risk of being exactly replicated and double-spent. This is the reason why previous attempts at a peer-to-peer electronic cash system required an intermediary of some sort - usually a financial institution - to prevent double-spending. The inclusion of an intermediary meant that these systems were centralized.

The more centralized money is, the more power the central authority accrues. This is what drives the desire and need for a decentralized electronic cash system. It's about distributing the power from the grip of a select few to the hands of the common people. This is the intuition behind the why of Bitcoin. The how of Bitcoin requires some further thought. The white paper goes in-depth and explains the process, but I'll attempt to summarize the two important underlying concepts of Bitcoin: the solution to the double-spending problem, and Proof of Work. But first, we must address the field of cryptography.

Key Pairs & SHA-256

Bitcoin makes use of two concepts from cryptography: public/private key pairs and cryptographic hash functions.

Every bitcoin owner is issued a public key with an associated private key. The private key is used to digitally sign transactions, such as sending or receiving bitcoin, and the public key is used to verify those transactions. It makes sense that the private key should be private information since it can authorize the transfer of important assets. The public key should be public information so that anyone who is a part of the decentralized network may verify that a transaction is legit.

A cryptographic hash function takes an arbitrary length of data input and produces a fixed-length output of bits called a hash. In the case of Bitcoin, a specific cryptographic hash function called SHA-256 is utilized and it outputs a value that is always 256 bits long. The purpose of a cryptographic hash function is to take some arbitrary data and form a concise identifier that is effectively unique to that piece of input data.

Transactions

An electronic coin represents a chain of digital signatures. Suppose that in the past, owner1 sent 1 BTC to owner2, and now, owner2 decides to send that same 1 BTC to owner3 such that

owner1 → owner2 → owner3

represents the sequential owner history of that 1 BTC. The following events occur when owner2 initiates the transfer of her cryptoasset to owner3. First, a new hash is formed such that

new_hash = sha256(pre_Tx, payee_public_key)

where pre_Tx is the data of the previous transaction (owner1 → owner2) and the payee_public_key is the public key of owner3. The new_hash serves as a compact, unique, identifier for the newly initiated transaction (owner2 → owner3). To authorize this transaction, the payer, owner2 in this case, digitally signs the new_hash using her private key such that

signature = sign(payer_private_key, new_hash).

Then, owner3 can verify the chain of ownership of that 1 BTC using the public key of owner2 such that

verification = verify(payer_public_key, new_hash).

Once signed, each transaction is broadcasted to the Bitcoin network where nodes (computers contributing their power to the network) are actively collecting the transactional data. The way double-spending is avoided is by the majority of nodes agreeing to accept only the first broadcasted transaction and ignoring any later attempts to double-spend.

As mentioned, an electronic coin represents a chain of digital signatures. As soon as the payer signs a single transaction, that transaction is broadcasted to the public and engrained in the chronological chain of the electronic coin, which now belongs to a new owner. So any later attempts by the payer to sign another transaction with the replicated electronic coin will be rejected by the network since the electronic coin now belongs to a new owner, meaning the payer no longer has authority over that cryptoasset.

Proof of Work

But how does the network decide on which transaction to accept first? The solution that Bitcoin proposes is to first group the transactions into timestamped blocks, then chain these blocks in chronological order in a way that it is immutable. This occurs by a process known as mining. Here's how it works.

Bitcoin initiates PoW (Proof of Work) by setting a target hash value, say target_hash, which is a 256-bit output starting with a specific number of zeros. Then, miners mine a new block such that

new_block = block(pre_hash, nonce, List<Tx>)

where pre_hash is the hash of the previous block, nonce is some arbitrary, adjustable, value, and List<Tx> is a list of transactions. The goal of the miners' is to increment the value of the nonce so that

sha256(new_block) == target_hash.

SHA-256 is an effective encryption mechanism because for any input, the unique output is completely unpredictable. That is, it is infeasible to find the input given the output of a SHA-256 function. As a result, finding the correct value of the new_block is computationally intensive since there's no better way to find it than to guess and check input values until one of them outputs the target_hash. But the idea is that it's easy to verify that the correct value of the new_block has been found. We just have to input it into the SHA-256 function and check to see that it outputs the target_hash.

This way of having an intense authentication step followed by a trivial verifaction step is by design. This means that a block cannot be changed without redoing the computational work for the block and all the following blocks since they each have chained information from the block before it. However, anyone can quickly verify the work put into authenticating a blockchain.

By convention, the Bitcoin network accepts the chain of blocks, or blockchain, that has the most computational work put into it and that's simply the longest one. So if an attacker tries to infiltrate the network with his own version of the blockchain, unless he has over 50% of the computing power of the entire Bitcoin network, statistically speaking, the chances of him forming the longest blockchain is extremely low.

The specific number of zeros of the target_hash changes periodically so that, on average, a new block is mined every 10 minutes. Since they provide the computing power, the miners are rewarded by the network in BTC. This is how new BTC is created or mined. At the time of this writing, the current circulating supply is about 18,915,618 BTC. Eventually, this number will be capped at 21,000,000 BTC, which then miners will be rewarded in fees paid by the users of the network.

This is Proof of Work. It's a way of authenticating, or proving, a blockchain through computational work. This is what allows transactions to be in chronological order, helping to solve the double-spending problem.

Network

Here's the general description of the Bitcoin protocol directly from the white paper:

  1. New transactions are broadcast to all nodes
  2. Each node collects new transactions into a block
  3. Each node works on finding a difficult proof-of-work for its block
  4. When a node finds a proof-of-work, it broadcasts the block to all nodes
  5. Nodes accept the block only if all transactions in it are valid and not already spent
  6. Nodes express their acceptance of the block by working on creating the next block in the chain, using the hash of the accepted block as the previous hash
